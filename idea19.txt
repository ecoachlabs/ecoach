Thought for 1m 25s
To get close to replacing an after-school teacher, your app has to understand one hard truth:

A topic is not learned when it has been explained. A topic is learned when the student can recognize it, understand it, use it, avoid common traps, and transfer it to a new form. That matches what evidence-based guidance emphasizes: learning strengthens when instruction is sequenced, when worked examples are alternated with practice, when retrieval is used to make knowledge more accessible, when feedback is targeted, and when students are taught to plan, monitor, and evaluate their own thinking. 

How a good teacher really teaches a topic
A strong teacher usually does not start with “content delivery.” They start with diagnosis. They try to find out:

what the student already knows,

what prerequisite knowledge is missing,

what misconceptions are already present,

whether the problem is vocabulary, concept, memory, reasoning, or procedure.

That matters because new learning depends heavily on prior knowledge, and the amount already stored in memory affects how much working memory is available for the next task. 

Then the teacher teaches the topic in a sequence like this:

1. Frame the destination
The teacher makes clear:

what this topic is,

why it matters,

what success looks like,

how it connects to older topics.

This reduces confusion and gives the learner a mental map. Good instruction is designed and sequenced, not dumped. 

2. Activate prior knowledge
Before new explanation, the teacher pulls up what the student should already know. That may be through a quick oral check, recall quiz, diagram labeling, or a warm-up problem. Retrieval is useful not only for remembering, but also for surfacing the knowledge needed to handle the current lesson with less strain on working memory. 

3. Explain the core idea in simple language
The teacher gives the central meaning first, not ten side notes. They answer:

What is it?

What is happening?

What are the key words?

What makes it different from similar things?

For many topics, this is where the teacher translates textbook language into student language. Deans for Impact notes that teaching should connect cognitive principles to practical instruction, and IES guidance recommends combining verbal explanation with supporting visuals. 

4. Model the thinking
This is the part many apps miss. The teacher does not only show the answer. The teacher shows the thinking path:

“First I notice…”

“This tells me the question is asking for…”

“I will ignore this irrelevant detail…”

“I choose this formula because…”

IES guidance specifically recommends using worked examples, talking through decision points, and then solving a similar problem with students. 

5. Move into guided practice
The student now tries, but with support:

prompts,

hints,

visual guides,

sentence stems,

step scaffolds,

teacher questioning.

The support should not stay forever. Guidance should gradually fade as competence grows. IES guidance explicitly recommends visual guides during early solving and then fading them over time so students do not become overly reliant on them. 

6. Check for understanding immediately
A real teacher keeps checking:

“Can you explain it back?”

“Which part confuses you?”

“Why is this option wrong?”

“Is this osmosis or diffusion?”

“What made you choose that?”

This is where the teacher catches fragile understanding before it hardens into a bad habit. Feedback is most useful when it improves the learner’s thinking and next step, not when it just announces right or wrong. 

7. Give independent practice with variation
Once the student has support, the teacher removes it and varies the form:

direct questions,

disguised questions,

diagram questions,

word problems,

compare/contrast,

real-life application,

past-question style,

time pressure.

IES guidance on mathematics recommends showing students how structurally similar problems can look different, include irrelevant information, or be presented through charts and diagrams. 

8. Revisit later
A good teacher does not “finish topic” and vanish. They come back to it after time has passed. IES recommends spacing learning over time and arranging review after delays of weeks or months, not just cramming it all at once. 

9. Interleave with nearby topics
A teacher also mixes related topics so the student learns discrimination:

when to use this idea,

when not to use it,

how it differs from neighboring concepts.

IES recommends interleaving worked examples and problem-solving, and this same logic applies more broadly to helping students distinguish among similar ideas. 

10. Build metacognition
At the strongest level, the teacher helps the student ask:

What is this question really testing?

What strategy fits?

Am I sure?

How do I check my work?

Why did I miss this?

EEF’s metacognition guidance emphasizes teaching students to plan, monitor, and evaluate their learning. 

How a student actually learns a topic
From the student side, learning a topic usually moves through these stages:

Exposure → meaning → recognition → guided use → independent use → correction → recall → transfer → fluency

That means a student has not truly learned a topic just because they can follow an explanation while it is fresh. Many students can recognize an idea during teaching but fail later when they must retrieve it independently. That is why retrieval practice and spaced review matter so much. 

A student truly knows a topic when they can:

define it in their own words,

spot it in different forms,

solve straightforward questions,

explain why wrong options are wrong,

handle traps and variations,

connect it to related topics,

retrieve it later without help. 

So what should your app do?
Your app should not behave like a PDF, note bank, or answer sheet.

It should behave like a topic tutor engine.

The job of the engine is to keep asking:

What exactly is weak here?
Not just: “Did the student get it wrong?”

Because a student can fail a topic for very different reasons:

they do not know the vocabulary,

they know the definition but cannot apply it,

they know the formula but cannot detect when to use it,

they understand slowly but accuracy collapses under speed,

they confuse sibling concepts,

they forget after 2 days,

they can solve when guided but not alone.

That kind of fine-grained diagnosis is the difference between “content app” and “teacher-like system.” It also matches your broader direction around wrong-answer intelligence and question-type intelligence.

There is one strategic caution, though. I would not market it too aggressively as “you do not need a teacher at all.” The evidence base is stronger for targeted tutoring, timely feedback, scaffolding, and self-regulated learning support than for total replacement of human support. One-to-one tuition is effective on average, but digital learning also depends heavily on self-regulation, and younger or lower-performing learners can disengage more easily if left too alone. This is the first major decision.

The coach should not pick the next mission randomly or by simple weakness alone.
It should use a Mission Priority Score.

Mission Priority Score
For each candidate topic or action, calculate:

Mission Priority = Weakness Weight + Urgency Weight + Curriculum Weight + Retention Weight + Opportunity Weight + Strategic Weight

Each component can be normalized to a 0–100 scale.

A. Weakness Weight
How academically weak is this area?

Inputs:

low mastery,

repeated wrong answers,

misconception recurrence,

low transfer performance,

poor confidence,

fragile recall.

Suggested scoring:

severe weakness: 80–100

fragile: 50–79

moderate concern: 25–49

stable: 0–24

B. Urgency Weight
How close is the relevant academic event?

Inputs:

days to quiz,

days to mock,

days to exam,

importance of event.

Suggested logic:

final exam near: very high

mock near: high

class test near: medium

no event soon: low

C. Curriculum Weight
How important is the topic in the syllabus structure?

Inputs:

prerequisite role,

topic centrality,

exam frequency,

cluster importance.

Suggested logic:

foundational, prerequisite-heavy topic: high

peripheral topic: lower

D. Retention Weight
Is this topic due for review or title defense?

Inputs:

days since last strong performance,

recall strength decay,

overdue review,

title defense due.

Suggested logic:

title defense due: very high

overdue review on fragile topic: high

recent stable review: low

E. Opportunity Weight
Can improvement here produce a strong result soon?

Inputs:

easy-to-fix weakness,

high-yield exam value,

recoverability,

leverage across other topics.

Example:
A topic that is weak but highly repairable before a mock should get a strong boost.

F. Strategic Weight
Does the broader plan currently need this?

Inputs:

current phase,

subject under pressure,

recent calendar changes,

post-mock recovery focus,

plan rebalancing.

This is where the plan asserts control.

3. Mission selection decision tree
After scoring, use a clear decision tree.

First check: Is there an emergency?
Emergency means:

title defense overdue and exam near,

major event within a few days and a critical topic is collapsing,

recent poor mock/test result requiring immediate repair,

strong calendar change forcing reprioritization.

If yes:

mission comes from emergency set first.

If no emergency:
Check in this order:

1. Is there a due or overdue strategic review?
Examples:

fragile topic due,

title defense due,

post-mock repair due.

If yes:

prioritize if score exceeds threshold.

2. Is there an exam-driven urgent weakness?
If a topic is highly relevant and close to an event:

prioritize it.

3. Is there a major misconception loop repeating?
If yes:

launch repair mission.

4. Is there a foundational gap blocking future progress?
If yes:

choose teach or foundation mission.

5. Is the learner ready for performance training?
If readiness is stable and timing is now the issue:

choose speed/performance mission.

6. Otherwise
Select the highest mission priority topic.

This ensures the coach behaves intelligently, not just reactively.

4. Session intent selection logic
Once a mission is chosen, the coach must decide the intent.

This is determined by the type of evidence attached to the topic.

Intent selection matrix
Teach
Use when:

mastery very low,

concept unseen or poorly understood,

repeated failure even on direct easy questions,

high hint dependency from the start.

Repair
Use when:

one specific misconception repeats,

one subskill keeps failing,

learner understands some of the topic but one pattern breaks repeatedly.

Strengthen
Use when:

correctness is inconsistent,

recall is shaky,

direct questions okay but application unstable,

mild fragility remains.

Firm Up
Use when:

topic mostly stable,

but performance under variation or timing is weak,

upcoming assessment is approaching,

repeated light errors remain.

Wrap Up
Use when:

assessment is near,

broad teaching is no longer efficient,

only high-yield finishing work should remain.

Perform
Use when:

knowledge is stable enough,

timing, pacing, and pressure are now the main concern,

exam realism matters.

Recover Confidence
Use when:

recent episode ended badly,

streak of poor results,

high hesitation,

student shows avoidance or demoralization signals.

Defend Title
Use when:

title is due for defense,

the topic matters strategically,

retention needs verifying.

Reclaim Title
Use when:

title is contested,

mastery slipped below threshold,

reclaiming that topic has strategic value.

5. Session intent scoring
To make this precise, compute intent scores.

For the selected topic, calculate:

Teach Score

Repair Score

Strengthen Score

Firm Up Score

Wrap Up Score

Perform Score

Recovery Score

Defense Score

Reclaim Score

Then choose the highest valid one.

Example formulas
Teach Score
High when:

mastery low,

direct easy accuracy low,

concept confidence low,

prerequisite gaps detected.

Repair Score
High when:

same misconception ID repeats,

errors cluster around one pattern,

general mastery not terrible, but one break is dominant.

Strengthen Score
High when:

moderate correctness,

unstable recall,

some success but not dependable,

help still needed sometimes.

Firm Up Score
High when:

exam proximity rising,

topic nearly stable,

transfer/timing still shaky,

fragility score moderate-high.

Wrap Up Score
High when:

event close,

topic not severely broken,

only selective finishing work remains.

Perform Score
High when:

mastery stable,

speed weak,

exam or mock very near,

learner ready for realistic pressure.

Recovery Score
High when:

confidence dropped sharply,

streak of poor episodes,

abandonment / avoidance increased,

performance worse than underlying knowledge suggests.

Defense Score
High when:

title active,

next defense due soon or overdue,

exam relevance high,

days since last verification high.

Reclaim Score
High when:

title contested,

recent evidence below title threshold,

reclaim would materially improve readiness.

This gives the coach a disciplined way to choose the kind of session.

6. Experience composition logic
After intent is selected, the coach must compose the exact session.

This is where the app becomes truly personalized.

Six main experience outputs
A. Timing Model
Chosen from:

untimed,

loose section timing,

standard timing,

per-question timing,

compressed timing.

Rules:

Teach / Recover → untimed or loose

Strengthen → untimed or light timing

Firm Up → moderate timing on part of session

Wrap Up → moderate or standard timing

Perform / Mock → standard or compressed timing

Defend Title → depends on topic profile and whether speed matters

B. Difficulty Curve
Chosen from:

easy ramp,

easy/medium,

balanced,

medium-heavy,

rising curve,

hard spike,

weakness-targeted curve.

Rules:

low confidence → easy ramp

overconfidence → disguised/trap-heavy earlier

title defense → balanced mixed verification

performance mode → realistic or slightly sharp

recovery → easier first wins, then rise

C. Question Mix
Possible components:

recall,

direct concept,

application,

disguised application,

past question,

misconception-targeted items,

transfer item,

timed fluency item.

Rules:

Teach → more concept + worked-example follow-ups

Repair → misconception-targeted

Strengthen → recall + application mix

Firm Up → recall + transfer + timing

Wrap Up → high-yield exam-like

Perform → exam-realistic mix

Title Defense → direct + disguised + application

D. Help Policy
Chosen from:

full support,

guided support,

limited hints,

rescue only,

no hints during run.

Rules:

Teach → full/guided

Repair → guided + targeted support

Strengthen → limited hints

Perform → no hints during official run

Defense → minimal or none during defense portion

E. Feedback Policy
Chosen from:

immediate full explanation,

immediate short feedback,

delayed to section end,

delayed to session end.

Rules:

Teach → immediate full

Repair → immediate targeted

Strengthen → immediate short or section-based

Perform / Mock → delayed

Defense → delayed until defense block ends

F. Pressure Level
Chosen from:

calm,

steady,

moderate,

high,

intense.

Rules:

recovery → calm

teaching → calm/steady

strengthening → steady/moderate

firming-up → moderate

wrap-up → moderate/high

performance → high if learner is ready

title defense → steady-serious, not playful

Badge categories

Badge rule structure
Each badge should require:

This keeps badges meaningful.

8. Title awarding logic
Titles should be much stricter than badges.

A topic title should only be awarded when mastery is convincingly demonstrated.

Title readiness model
For each title-eligible topic, calculate a Title Readiness Score.

Components of Title Readiness
Correctness Stability

Hint Independence

Transfer Strength

Recall Retention

Speed Stability

Error Cleanliness

Multi-Session Consistency

Example title formula
Title Readiness = 25% Correctness Stability + 15% Hint Independence + 20% Transfer Strength + 15% Recall Retention + 10% Speed Stability + 5% Error Cleanliness + 10% Multi-Session Consistency

You can adjust the weights by topic type.

Interpretation
below 60 → not ready

60–74 → candidate

75–84 → strong candidate

85+ → title award possible

Hard gates for title award
Even with a high score, require minimum gates such as:

at least two successful sessions,

at least one delayed success or review confirmation,

at least one application or transfer success,

low hint dependence in final verification,

no major recurring misconception unresolved.

That prevents shallow titles.

9. Title defense scheduling logic
Now the boxing system becomes real.

A title should not be defended randomly.
It should be scheduled by logic.

Title Defense Pressure Score
Compute:

Defense Pressure = Time Since Last Verification + Topic Importance + Exam Proximity + Slippage Risk + Fragility Trend

A. Time Since Last Verification
Longer time increases defense pressure.

B. Topic Importance
Core, high-yield topics should be defended more often.

C. Exam Proximity
As exams approach, strategic titles should be re-verified sooner.

D. Slippage Risk
If recent weak signals appear, defense should come earlier.

E. Fragility Trend
If recall strength is dipping, pressure rises.

Defense schedule examples
Low-risk title
defend every few weeks or at major checkpoints

Medium-risk title
defend sooner, especially before mocks

High-value exam title
defend during firming-up and again before performance phase

Contested title
reclaim mission first, then re-defense soon after

10. Title defense outcomes
A defense session should produce a clear result.

Defended
The learner passes confidently.

title remains active

next defense pushed out

Narrowly Defended
Learner passes, but signs of instability remain.

title remains active

earlier next defense

optional maintenance review added

Contested
Learner fails enough evidence checks.

title moves to contested

reclaim mission scheduled

Dormant
If the title is neglected for too long or repeatedly contested.

title moves to dormant

reclaim path required later

This makes titles alive, not decorative.

11. Title reclaim logic
When a title is contested, the coach should decide whether reclaim is urgent.

Reclaim Priority Score
Compute from:

exam proximity,

topic importance,

severity of slippage,

learner confidence impact,

mission opportunity value.

If reclaim priority is high:

schedule reclaim mission soon.

Reclaim structure
A reclaim mission should usually be:

narrower than title earning,

focused on the actual slippage point,

followed by a short reclaim check.

If reclaim succeeds:

title becomes reclaimed

then returns to active with closer next defense

12. Review scheduling logic
Every session should also feed the review engine.

Review urgency should depend on:
fragility,

improvement significance,

upcoming events,

whether a title was just awarded,

whether a misconception was just repaired.

Example rules
If a topic was just repaired but remains fragile:

schedule quick review soon

If a topic was strengthened significantly:

schedule moderate spacing

If a title was earned:

schedule defense window later

If a topic is stable and low-risk:

schedule lighter maintenance

This means review is dynamically shaped by the coach, not static.

11. Badge and Title rules
Now the rule layer.

Badges: when they are earned
Badges reward meaningful movement, not mere usage.

Badge earning conditions
A badge should require:

a clear trigger,

enough evidence,

a meaningful delta,

not already awarded too recently.

Core badge families
Breakthrough badges
Earn when a repeated weakness is genuinely reduced.
Examples:

Wording Breakthrough

Diagram Breakthrough

Formula Recall Breakthrough

Recovery badges
Earn when a learner bounces back from poor performance.
Examples:

Comeback Session

Confidence Rebuilt

Recovery Win

Performance badges
Earn when execution improves.
Examples:

Fast and Accurate

Pressure Stabilizer

Transfer Breakthrough

Discipline badges
Earn for behavior patterns.
Examples:

Three-Day Return

Weekly Discipline

Review Keeper

Badge rule examples
Wording Breakthrough
Award if:

wording confusion was previously recurring,

learner now succeeds on at least 3 relevant items,

hint dependence lower than prior baseline.

Comeback Session
Award if:

previous episode ended poorly,

current recovery episode shows significant positive delta,

accuracy and/or confidence materially improved.

Fast and Accurate
Award if:

speed improves beyond threshold,

correctness stays above target,

confirmed across more than one timed episode.

Titles: when they are earned
Titles should be rare enough to feel important.

Title readiness criteria
A title should only be awarded if:

accuracy is strong,

help dependence is low,

more than one question form is passed,

at least one delayed or later confirmation exists,

transfer/application is demonstrated,

major misconception is resolved.

Suggested title states
Candidate

Earned

Active

Defense Due

Defended

Contested

Dormant

Reclaimed

Title award rule example
Award Ratio Champion if:

direct items passed,

disguised items passed,

application passed,

help use below threshold,

at least 2 separate successful episodes,

no unresolved major misconception in ratio setup.

Candidate logic
If near but not fully proven:

mark as Title Candidate

schedule confirmation session instead of immediate title award.

When a title becomes contested
A title should become contested if:

defense fails below threshold,

repeated slippage appears in later sessions,

major misconception reappears strongly,

recall or transfer breaks materially.

Tone should be:

Your title is contested, not erased.
The core knowledge is still there, but no longer stable enough.

When a title is defended
A title is defended if:

defense set is passed at target threshold,

no catastrophic breakdown in key component.

Possible defense outcomes:

Defended

Narrowly Defended

Contested

Narrowly defended
Means:

title remains active,

next defense comes sooner,

maintenance mission is scheduled.

When a title is reclaimed
A reclaim should happen after:

title contested,

targeted reclaim mission completed,

reclaim check passed.

After reclaim:

state becomes Reclaimed, then Active

next defense scheduled sooner than normal

12. Missed-session logic
This needs careful rules.

A missed session should not always trigger the same response.

Session states
scheduled

upcoming

started

completed

partially completed

missed

rescheduled

excused

Missed session thresholds
A session becomes “missed” if:

not started within window,

started but abandoned below threshold,

not rescheduled appropriately.

Coach reaction to one missed session
Usually:

gentle reminder,

offer reschedule,

note effect on plan.

Coach reaction to repeated missed sessions
Usually:

alert parent,

reduce strategy optimism,

re-evaluate consistency risk,

possibly shorten or re-time missions.

Coach reaction near an exam
More serious:

stronger alerts,

re-compression of strategy,

lost-day handling,

subject reprioritization.

13. Parent alert escalation ladder
Not every missed session should disturb the parent the same way.

Use an escalation ladder.

Level 1 — silent student-only reminder
For one minor miss.

Level 2 — student reminder + parent visible in dashboard
For repeated misses or strategic session missed.

Level 3 — active parent alert
For:

2–3 missed sessions in a short period,

title defense missed,

critical pre-mock sessions missed,

steep drop in consistency.

Level 4 — urgent parent escalation
For:

multiple critical misses close to exam,

severe disengagement,

drastic schedule slippage.

This avoids noise while preserving accountability.

7. Reminder, missed-session, and escalation rules
Now the operational logic.

A. Reminder ladder for the student
Reminder 1: upcoming reminder
Sent before session.
Example:

Coach session in 30 minutes: Ratio Repair

Reminder 2: start-time alarm
At scheduled time.
Example:

Your coach session is ready now.

Reminder 3: gentle overdue reminder
If not started within a grace window.
Example:

You haven’t started today’s session yet.

Reminder 4: recovery prompt
If session is effectively missed.
Example:

Today’s session was missed. A recovery slot has been suggested.

B. Missed-session classification
A session can end as:

completed

partially completed

paused

rescheduled

missed

excused

Missed
Not started or not substantially completed in expected window.

Excused
Parent or student marked a valid conflict.

Partially completed
Useful if work began, but evidence insufficient.

This distinction matters because strategy should react differently.

C. Parent escalation ladder
Level 0 — no parent alert
Small harmless miss, low urgency.

Level 1 — passive visibility
Appears in Parent Overview / Alert Center only.

Use when:

one ordinary session missed,

no urgent exam pressure.

Level 2 — active parent alert
Push or strong in-app alert.

Use when:

repeated miss,

critical repair session missed,

title defense missed,

exam is close.

Level 3 — urgent escalation
Very prominent alert.

Use when:

multiple critical misses close to mock/final,

severe drop in consistency,

high strategic risk.

D. Escalation rules
If 1 low-importance session missed
remind student

show passive notice to parent

If 2+ sessions missed in short window
parent active alert

strategy optimism reduced

mission lengths may shorten

If critical pre-exam session missed
parent active alert

reschedule automatically

plan may compress

If title defense missed
mark title “defense overdue”

notify parent if strategically important

reschedule defense or shift to contested risk soon

If repeated misses continue
raise engagement risk

simplify plan

prioritize only highest-yield missions

increase parent visibility

9. Key event flows
Now the most important full-system flows.

Flow A: normal coach session
mission_generated

user taps start → mission_start_requested

blueprint created → mission_started

step shown → step_rendered

learner answers → step_answer_submitted

evaluator detects issue → maybe misconception_detected

if needed → intervention_triggered

user enters detour → intervention_started

detour completed → intervention_completed

user returns → mission_returned_from_intervention

mission finishes → mission_completed

evidence summarized → topic_progress_updated

reward decision maybe → badge_awarded or title_candidate_created

follow-up scheduled → review_scheduled

Flow B: title defense
defense becomes due → title_defense_due

coach surfaces it in Hub/Titles Hall

user starts defense → title_defense_started

defense steps run

result computed:

title_defended

or title_narrowly_defended

or title_contested

if contested → title_reclaim_scheduled

Coach Hub and Parent Overview rerender

Flow C: calendar change
academic_event_updated

Replanning engine recalculates

strategy_version_changed or plan_adjusted (add this event)

new mission priorities generated

updated reminders scheduled

parent_alert_generated if needed

Coach Hub shows plan update banner

Flow D: parent feedback changes strategy
parent_feedback_submitted

interpretation engine produces parent_feedback_interpreted

plan engine adjusts sessions and/or timing

parent_feedback_applied

student receives softer/harder or rescheduled plan

parent gets action summary message

Flow E: missed session escalation
reminder scheduled and sent

session not started

session_marked_missed

EngagementStore updates

if threshold crossed → engagement_risk_increased

recovery mission generated

maybe parent_alert_generated

Coach Hub and Parent Overview update

12. Minimum reusable props/state contracts
You do not need full code yet, but you should enforce contracts.

Every major card should have:

Visual contract
title

context label

severity or tone

body content

action list

Behavior contract
listens to store slice

emits named events

no hidden business logic inside component

Persistence contract
if it changes user learning state, it must produce a domain event

That last rule is important.
UI should never silently mutate academic logic.

14. The most important implementation law
Here it is:

Every meaningful coaching action must be evented, stored, and explainable.

That means:

why the mission was chosen,

why the intervention happened,

why the title was awarded,

why the parent was alerted,

why the plan changed.

This is what makes the system feel intelligent rather than arbitrary.

A reusable component system
