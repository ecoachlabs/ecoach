Was the diagnosis actually right?

Good. Here is the Content Operating System Specification.

The right way to think about this is: content is not a library attached to the coach; it is an operating system that absorbs raw materials, extracts structure, packages instructional assets, scores trust and usefulness, detects gaps, feeds the brain, and then learns from usage outcomes. Your source files already define eight permanent content domains, a canonical content ontology, content entities under a dedicated cx_ namespace, a lifecycle model, a usage feedback loop, and explicit cross-engine relationships to Coverage, Hypothesis Competition, Timing, Risk, Adaptation, Session Composer, and Governance. 

 

 


1. Purpose of the Content OS
The Content OS exists to answer six hard questions continuously:

What raw educational material do we have?

What structure can we extract from it?

What coach-usable artifacts can we package from it?

Is the content good enough, trustworthy enough, and deep enough?

What content is missing for the learner’s current state and proof needs?

Which artifacts actually work in practice, for which learners, under which conditions? 

 


That is why the docs treat content intelligence as a first-class fabric rather than a side utility. They explicitly say the coach cannot depend only on pre-existing content and needs a content engine that can understand curriculum, map topics to concept atoms, fetch or generate notes, create discriminative questions, contrast drills, pressure probes, proof tasks, retrieval cards, rate quality, and select content by learner state. 

 


2. Non-negotiable doctrine
A. Content must be structurally aligned
Every source must be absorbed into the coach’s structural world model, not stored as loose files. The docs define source ontology, knowledge object ontology, artifact ontology, representation ontology, and proof-support ontology precisely so content can plug into the rest of the brain cleanly. 


B. Content must be coach-usable, not merely stored
The system should not stop at “PDF uploaded” or “text extracted.” It has to package note artifacts, explanations, worked examples, drills, assessments, contrast artifacts, formula cards, glossary cards, recap artifacts, assignments, pressure probes, and delayed-recall assets. 


C. Content must carry trust and quality
The docs explicitly separate source trust, extraction confidence, ambiguity, instructional usefulness, representation coverage, exam alignment, and misconception coverage as quality dimensions. They also make low-trust or suspect artifacts blockable from high-stakes use. 

 


D. Missing content must be explicit
A critical doctrine in the corpus is that the brain must know when it lacks content rather than silently improvising poor substitutes. That is why cx_content_requests exists. 


E. Content must be judged by outcome, not presence
The lifecycle and feedback sections are explicit: content that confuses learners or fails to support proof should not remain permanently active just because it exists. 


3. Permanent domains of the Content OS
I would lock the eight permanent domains exactly as the docs imply, but make them operational.

Domain A — Source Absorption
This domain ingests curriculum guides, syllabi, textbooks, class notes, past questions, marking schemes, glossary sheets, formula sheets, teacher-authored materials, and generated resources. It owns provenance, versioning, fingerprints, and raw source registration. 


Domain B — Structural Extraction
This domain extracts subjects, topics, concepts, sub-concepts, formulas, definitions, worked steps, diagrams, examples, question-answer alignments, and misconception cues from raw materials. 


Domain C — Knowledge Packaging
This domain turns extracted material into concept notes, formula cards, glossary entries, misconception entries, prerequisite links, representation variants, and example families. 


Domain D — Assessment Packaging
This domain creates or organizes question items, probes, timed sets, mixed-context sets, mastery checks, recall checks, contrast drills, and error-correction drills. 


Domain E — Instructional Packaging
This domain builds lesson blocks, worked examples, explanation variants, contrast sheets, retrieval packs, recap cards, memory anchors, and assignment bundles. 


Domain F — Content Quality and Trust
This domain rates source trust, extraction confidence, ambiguity, instructional usefulness, representation coverage, exam alignment, and misconception coverage. 


Domain G — Content Need and Gap Detection
This domain finds missing topic content, missing representation coverage, missing proof-support artifacts, weak question diversity, missing misconception repair materials, insufficient timed material, and weak delayed-recall assets. 


Domain H — Content Governance and Feedback
This domain tracks which artifacts were used, whether they worked, when they should be retired, when a generated artifact must be revised, and when source trust should be downgraded.

8. Content sufficiency doctrine
