Thought for 7s
What you are describing is a very important layer of learning intelligence:

academic decay is the gradual weakening of what a student once knew, while forgetfulness or recall failure is the moment that weakness shows up when the student tries to bring the knowledge back.

They are related, but they are not exactly the same.

The distinction
Academic decay means the learning itself is fading, fragmenting, or losing stability over time.

Recall failure means the knowledge may still exist partly in the mind, but the student cannot retrieve it when needed.

So a student can face at least four different states:

True mastery
They know it and can recall it under different conditions.

Stored but fragile knowledge
They learned it before, but it is weak and may disappear under time pressure, stress, or slight question variation.

Recognition without recall
They can recognize the answer when they see options, but cannot produce it from memory on their own.

False familiarity
They think they know it because it feels familiar, but they cannot explain, apply, or retrieve it correctly.

That distinction matters because many systems treat all wrong answers the same, but they are not the same problem.

What academic decay really looks like
Academic decay does not only mean “the student forgot.” It can mean:

Trace decay
The memory trace becomes weak because it has not been revisited.

Interference decay
New learning disrupts older learning. Similar concepts begin to clash.

Retrieval pathway collapse
The student may still know it, but the mental route to access it is weak.

Context dependency
They know it in one setting but cannot recall it in a different wording, environment, or pressure level.

Speed collapse
They know it when relaxed, but recall breaks under time pressure.

Confidence distortion
They hesitate too long, second-guess themselves, and lose access to what they actually know.

Fragmentation
They remember bits and pieces but not the full structure.

Application decay
They remember the definition but cannot use it in a question.

So the fight is not just against forgetting facts. It is against the breakdown of usable knowledge.

The real enemy is not only forgetting
In education, the enemy is broader:

1. Weak encoding
The topic was never deeply understood in the first place.

2. Poor consolidation
It was learned once but not stabilized.

3. Retrieval neglect
The student keeps rereading instead of pulling from memory.

4. Similar-topic confusion
Concept A and Concept B contaminate each other.

5. Pressure-induced recall failure
Stress blocks access.

6. Knowledge isolation
The student knows the idea alone, but not in relation to other ideas.

7. Lack of varied retrieval
The student only practiced in one format.

8. Illusion of mastery
They mistake familiarity for retention.

A sharper way to frame it
You could describe the whole problem as:

The erosion of stable academic memory and the struggle to preserve, recover, and reliably retrieve learning when it is needed.

Or more simply:

Academic decay is the weakening of learned knowledge over time; the fight is to preserve recall, rebuild access, and make memory usable under real exam conditions.

Academic Decay and Recall Resilience Engine
This engine exists to answer one core question:

What exactly is fading, why is it fading, how serious is it, and what should the coach do right now to stop the student from losing usable knowledge?

This is not just a revision feature.
It is a memory-defense intelligence system inside the coach.

Its job is to continuously watch learning, detect decay before collapse, separate true forgetting from retrieval blockage, and trigger the right repair path.

1. Core mission of the engine
The engine should protect learning across five fronts:

Retention
Does the student still have the knowledge?

Recall
Can the student bring it out without help?

Stability
Can the student still use it after time passes?

Transfer
Can the student recall it when the form changes?

Pressure survival
Can the student still retrieve it under timing, stress, or mixed-topic conditions?

So the engine is not satisfied with “the child once got it right.”

It wants to know:

Can they still recall it three days later?

Can they explain it?

Can they distinguish it from similar concepts?

Can they apply it in a new question form?

Can they retrieve it quickly enough in exam conditions?

5. What “decay” should mean technically
Decay should not just mean “has not revised recently.”

The engine should compute several kinds of decay.

5.1 Time Decay
The longer since last successful retrieval, the higher the risk.

But raw time alone is too crude.

A concept recalled successfully 9 times should decay differently from one recalled once.

5.2 Stability Decay
This measures whether successful recall has become unstable over repeated checks.

A concept may still be “known,” but if performance is oscillating, it is fragile.

5.3 Speed Decay
The student may still get the answer right, but recall time is worsening.

That means exam usefulness is decaying.

5.4 Transfer Decay
The student remembers only the familiar version.

As soon as wording or format changes, performance drops.

5.5 Pressure Decay
The concept survives in calm conditions but collapses under timing.

5.6 Structure Decay
The student remembers isolated facts but not how they connect.

5.7 Interference Decay
A nearby concept is corrupting access to the target concept.

Each type of decay should be tracked separately, not lumped together.

Each unit can have a profile like this:

7. Diagnostic questions the engine should answer
For every weak concept, the coach should be able to ask:

Was this ever truly mastered?

Is the student failing free recall or all forms of recall?

Is the problem time-based decay or interference?

Is retrieval slow, blocked, or inaccurate?

Does the student perform better with cues?

Does performance collapse under time pressure?

Is this concept isolated or connected to a wider misunderstanding?

Does a similar concept consistently intrude?

Is the student forgetting the content or forgetting how to access it?

What is the cheapest high-impact intervention right now?

That last question is important.
The coach should avoid overreacting.

8. Intervention logic
Here is how the engine should think.

Case A: Recognition present, free recall absent
Student can choose the answer but cannot produce it.

This means memory exists weakly but retrieval is not independent.

Use:

hidden-answer recall

fill-the-gap recall

verbal retrieval

cue fading

short repeated free recall attempts

Do not rely on more multiple choice.

Case B: Free recall present, applied recall weak
Student can state the definition but cannot solve with it.

Use:

worked example bridge

classify-then-solve drills

identify-which-rule-applies tasks

context variation practice

Case C: Untimed success, timed collapse
Student knows it but recall speed is too slow.

Use:

compressed recall drills

time-boxed retrieval bursts

escalating pace rounds

small timed sets before full tests

Case D: Similar-topic confusion
Student keeps mixing two related concepts.

Use:

contrast tables

“which is which” drills

paired examples

error explanation

deliberate discrimination tasks

Case E: Learned before, now fading
This is classic reactivation territory.

Use:

no-option recall

minimal cue trigger

short recovery cycle

spaced rechecks

Do not reteach fully unless recovery fails.

Case F: Fragmented knowledge
Student remembers disconnected parts.

Use:

concept map reconstruction

sequence rebuilding

step ordering

explain-from-start-to-finish drills

Case G: Confidence collapse
Student knows but freezes or overthinks.

Use:

confidence-calibration drills

fast-first-response practice

second-guess tracking

low-stakes speed rounds

repeated success under pressure

Step 1: detect weak performance or decay risk
Step 2: classify the memory state
Step 3: identify failure mode
Step 4: estimate severity
Step 5: pick lowest-cost effective intervention
Step 6: retest in same mode
Step 7: retest in harder mode

11. Priority logic
The engine should decide what to repair first using a formula like:

Priority = importance × exam relevance × decay severity × recurrence risk × dependency weight

A small forgotten fact may matter less than a fragile prerequisite concept.

For example, if the student is weak in:

basic algebraic manipulation

fractions

place value

a minor science definition

The system should rescue the prerequisite first because it damages many downstream topics.

So the engine must understand dependency chains.

12. Dependency-aware decay

Examples:

The engine should mark such units as high-leverage memory anchors.

If these decay, the system should escalate quickly.

Internal logic
The engine should not use one crude formula.
It should calculate several partial risk components, then combine them.

A. Time-based weakening
If a concept was learned once and not retrieved for 10 days, that is risky.
If it was successfully retrieved 12 times and not seen for 10 days, less risky.

So raw time must be weighted by:

strength of prior mastery

recency of successful recall

concept difficulty

student-specific forgetting speed

B. Stability trend
A concept may still look “known,” but if recent attempts are:

success

hesitation success

fail

success

fail

then it is unstable.

C. Retrieval latency drift
If accuracy remains high but answer time keeps increasing, that is an early warning sign.

D. Support drift
If the student now needs hints/options for something they once recalled freely, that is decay.

E. Mode narrowing
If the student used to succeed in application but now only succeeds in recognition, that is decay.

F. Pressure collapse
If calm recall remains intact but timed recall drops sharply, pressure decay is occurring.

State transitions
The Decay Detection Engine should move units through a state ladder:

Stable → no immediate concern

Watchlist → early warning signs

Fragile → weakening evident

Decaying → repeated weakening/failures

Collapsed → reliable retrieval no longer present

A concept can also recover upward through:

collapsed → fragile

fragile → watchlist

watchlist → stable

Recovery should require actual evidence, not just one lucky correct answer.

Trigger events
This engine should run when:

a retrieval attempt finishes

a spaced review is due

a pressure session ends

a mixed-topic quiz reveals old-topic errors

a confusion incident is detected

a concept has been idle longer than its safe interval

Intervention implications

2. Recall Mode Classification Engine
Purpose
This engine answers:

What kind of remembering is the student actually demonstrating?

A lot of systems mistake recognition for mastery.
This engine prevents that.

Core insight
There is a big difference between:

“I remember it”

“I can recognize it”

“I can say it without help”

“I can explain it”

“I can use it”

“I can still use it when conditions change”

This engine classifies the student’s memory mode.

Inputs
It consumes evidence from multiple interaction types:

multiple choice questions

fill-in-the-gap tasks

typed free response

oral recall

worked examples

explain-in-your-own-words tasks

step ordering tasks

reverse questions

transfer questions

timed questions

It also uses:

hints required

time to answer

number of retries

prompt strength needed

Outputs
For each knowledge unit, it should emit:

dominant_recall_mode

highest_verified_recall_mode

weakest_successful_mode

mode_gap_profile

Possible recall modes:

exposure only

familiarity only

recognition

cued recall

free recall

reconstructed recall

applied recall

transfer recall

pressure-stable recall

Internal logic
The engine should classify by evidence, not assumption.

Example
If the student:

gets multiple choice right

fails fill-in

fails oral definition

gets it right after a hint

then the mode is likely:
recognition + weak cued recall

Another student:

states the formula correctly

solves direct questions

fails when the variables are rearranged

then the mode is:
free recall stable, transfer recall weak

Another student:

explains the concept clearly

solves variants correctly

fails timed bursts

then the mode is:
applied recall present, pressure-stable recall absent

State transitions
A typical growth path is:

exposed

familiar

recognizable

cued recall

free recall

applied recall

transfer recall

pressure-stable recall

durable recall

A decay path may reverse that.

That means the engine also helps detect how far down the ladder a student has slipped.

Trigger events
Run this engine whenever:

a question is answered

a recall drill is completed

a concept explanation task is completed

a timed set finishes

the same concept is observed in different formats

Why it matters
Because the coach must not prescribe the same response to:

recognition-only memory

free-recall weakness

application weakness

pressure weakness

Those are different problems.

Intervention implications
The engine should attach recommendations like:

move_from_recognition_to_free_recall

move_from_free_recall_to_application

move_from_application_to_transfer

pressure_stabilization_required

3. Failure Diagnosis Engine
Purpose
This is one of the most important engines in the system.

It answers:

Why is the student failing right now?

Not every failure means forgetting.
This engine separates failure causes.

Failure causes it should detect
At minimum:

true forgetting

retrieval blockage

concept interference

incomplete original understanding

context dependency

pressure-induced collapse

confidence disruption

fragmented knowledge

surface familiarity without depth

procedural breakdown

misread / attention error

working memory overload

Inputs
The engine should use:

question content

concept tags

concept neighbors

student answer

correctness

latency

hesitation markers

hint usage

prior success history

prior failure history

mode classification

timed vs untimed performance

error patterns

similarity map

pressure profile

switching behavior

step-level logs, if procedural task

Outputs
It should emit:

primary_failure_mode

secondary_failure_mode

diagnostic_confidence

evidence_trace

intervention_family

Examples:

primary_failure_mode = interference

secondary_failure_mode = low confidence

intervention_family = contrast drill + confidence stabilization

Internal reasoning logic
A. True forgetting
Likely when:

prior mastery was weak or moderate

long time gap

failures across all modes

no recovery even with cue

no strong sign of competing concept intrusion

B. Retrieval blockage
Likely when:

student says “I know this”

strong hesitation

answer emerges after small cue

recognition succeeds but free recall fails

prior success exists recently

C. Interference
Likely when:

wrong answer matches nearby concept

confusion pair already known

errors cluster around sibling topic

performance drops after learning similar material

D. Incomplete understanding
Likely when:

concept was never strong historically

explanation is shallow

application has always been weak

student memorized wording but cannot manipulate idea

E. Context lock
Likely when:

standard textbook phrasing succeeds

reworded question fails

real-world application fails

reverse framing fails

F. Pressure breakdown
Likely when:

untimed success high

timed success low

latency spikes under pressure

second-guessing increases in timed mode

G. Confidence disruption
Likely when:

student changes right answer to wrong

takes too long on easy material

answers correctly after first instinct in review

low confidence despite repeated correctness

H. Fragmentation
Likely when:

bits are recalled but not connected

student knows formula but not variables

knows steps 1 and 3 but misses step 2

partial responses recur

State model
Failure diagnosis can be tracked at two levels:

Per attempt
What likely happened on this question?

Per concept
What is the dominant recurring cause of failure for this concept?

That distinction matters.
A concept may have one bad attempt due to attention, but over time reveal an interference pattern.

Trigger events
Run this engine whenever:

a significant miss happens

a concept relapses after prior mastery

a timed/untimed gap appears

a confusion pattern emerges

a student repeatedly succeeds only with support

Intervention mapping
This engine should map directly to intervention families:

forgetting → reactivation or rebuild

retrieval blockage → cue fading + forced recall

interference → contrast drills

incomplete understanding → re-teach from structure

context lock → variation drills

pressure breakdown → timed ladder

confidence disruption → confidence calibration

fragmentation → reconstruction drills

4. Memory State Engine
Purpose
This engine is the source of truth for where each knowledge unit currently stands.

It answers:

What is the present memory condition of this concept for this student?

Inputs
It consumes outputs from:

decay detection

recall mode classification

failure diagnosis

past attempts

success consistency

last retrieval times

pressure performance

transfer performance

Outputs
A single canonical memory state per knowledge unit.

Suggested state ladder:

unexposed

exposed

familiar

recognizable

cued-recallable

free-recallable

applicable

transferable

pressure-stable

durable

You may also add flags:

fragile

recovering

relapsing

interference-prone

pressure-vulnerable

Internal logic
This engine should be conservative.

A student should not be promoted to a higher state because of one isolated success.
The system should require repeated evidence.

Example rules
To reach free-recallable, the student may need:

at least 2 independent successful free recalls

low hint dependency

no recent contradiction

To reach applicable, the student may need:

successful use in direct question solving

not just definition recall

To reach transferable, the student may need:

success across different phrasings or contexts

To reach pressure-stable, the student may need:

success in timed conditions

latency within acceptable range

no major confidence collapse

To reach durable, the student may need:

success preserved after meaningful time gaps

State transitions
The engine should explicitly support both upward and downward moves.

Example upward path
recognizable → cued recallable → free recallable → applicable

Example downward path
pressure-stable → applicable only → recognizable

This is critical because decay is not binary.
Knowledge slips through layers.

Trigger events
Recompute state when:

any concept attempt ends

a review cycle completes

a spaced interval passes

a transfer probe is done

a pressure probe is done

Why this engine matters
Without a clear memory state engine, the system becomes vague.
You need one place that says:

“This student is not weak in this topic generally.
They are at recognition with fragile cued recall on this concept.”

That level of specificity changes everything.

5. Interference Mapping Engine
Purpose
This engine tracks concept collisions.

It answers:

Which knowledge units are corrupting or confusing each other?

This is one of the most valuable engines in academic memory defense.

Why it matters
A student often has not “forgotten” one idea completely.
They are retrieving the wrong neighboring idea.

Examples:

perimeter instead of area

osmosis instead of diffusion

denominator instead of numerator

adjective instead of adverb

mean instead of median

evaporation instead of boiling

Inputs
It should consume:

incorrect answers

wrong-option patterns

concept tags of wrong answers

topic adjacency

curriculum proximity

semantic similarity

student history of confusion

order in which concepts were learned

time between learning related concepts

explanation text, if available

Outputs
It should build a confusion graph with outputs such as:

interference_neighbors[]

confusion_direction

confusion_strength_score

confusion_context

confusion_frequency

most_common_intruder_concept

recommended_disambiguation_set

Internal logic
This engine should not only say concept A is confused with B.

It should also know:

does A intrude into B more than B intrudes into A?

does confusion occur only under time pressure?

does confusion occur only in application mode?

did confusion begin after a new topic was introduced?

Example
Student was fine on “simile” until “metaphor” was introduced.
Now metaphor appears as the wrong answer repeatedly.

That is not ordinary forgetting.
That is curriculum-triggered interference.

State model
Each pair of confusable concepts can carry a relationship state:

unrelated

weak overlap

watchlist pair

active confusion pair

high-risk confusion pair

stabilized pair

Trigger events
Run or update this engine when:

a wrong answer maps strongly to another known concept

contrast exercises are completed

timed confusion spikes happen

Intervention implications
This engine should feed interventions like:

mixed discrimination sets

reverse examples

4. Derived feature extraction layer
Before scoring, the system should derive higher-level features from raw attempts.

These are the main derived features.

4.1 Accuracy features
For a given knowledge unit and recent time window:

recent accuracy

rolling accuracy

mode-specific accuracy

partial correctness ratio

consistency score

Formula:

recent_accuracy = weighted_mean(rawScore over last N attempts, newer attempts higher weight)
consistency_score = 1 - normalized_variance(rawScore over last N attempts)
4.2 Latency features
You should not use raw time directly because different question types differ.

Use normalized response time:

normalized_latency = responseTimeMs / expectedResponseTimeMsFor(mode, difficulty, format)
Then convert into a quality score:

latency_quality = clamp(1 - ((normalized_latency - 1) / latencyTolerance), 0, 1)
Interpretation:

1.0 = very fast or expected

0.5 = somewhat slow

0.0 = very slow / collapsed access

4.3 Support dependency features
support_dependency = weighted combination of:
  hints_used_rate
  options_visible_dependency
  formula_bank_dependency
  success_with_support - success_without_support gap
Possible formula:

support_dependency_score =
  35 * hints_dependency_rate +
  30 * options_dependency_gap +
  20 * formula_support_gap +
  15 * cue_recovery_only_rate
Scaled to 0–100.

High score means the student is leaning on support.

4.4 Confidence calibration features
This measures whether confidence matches reality.

confidence_error = mean(abs(confidenceSelfReport - rawScore))
confidence_calibration_score = 100 * (1 - confidence_error)
You also want:

right-to-wrong switching rate

low-confidence-on-correct rate

high-confidence-on-wrong rate

These help detect confidence disruption.

4.5 Pressure gap features
This is very important.

pressure_gap = calm_mode_performance - pressured_mode_performance
Where performance is a weighted combination of:

accuracy

latency quality

switching behavior

freeze markers

Example:

mode_performance =
  0.50 * accuracy +
  0.25 * latency_quality +
  0.15 * (1 - switch_rate) +
  0.10 * (1 - freeze_rate)
Then:

pressure_gap_score = 100 * max(0, calm_mode_performance - pressure_mode_performance)
4.6 Interference features
For each incorrect attempt, check whether the wrong answer maps to a known sibling or contrast-pair concept.

Then compute:

interference_rate =
  confusion-linked wrong attempts / total wrong attempts
Also compute:

timed interference rate

specific intruder dominance

directional confusion ratio

5. Decay Detection Engine
Exact scoring model
Decay should be a composite score.

Use six components.

decay_risk_score =
  0.22 * time_decay +
  0.18 * stability_decay +
  0.16 * latency_decay +
  0.14 * support_decay +
  0.15 * mode_decay +
  0.15 * pressure_decay
All scaled 0–100.

5.1 Time decay
This must respect prior mastery.

days_gap = days_since(lastSuccessAt)
mastery_buffer = 1 + (successful_independent_recalls * 0.35)

time_decay_raw = days_gap / mastery_buffer
time_decay = 100 * sigmoid((time_decay_raw - safeGapThreshold) / gapSlope)
Where:

safeGapThreshold depends on unit difficulty and history

harder concepts decay faster

durable units decay slower

A simple version:

safeGapThresholdDays =
  2 + (independent_success_count * 1.5) + (memory_state_bonus_days)
5.2 Stability decay
This captures oscillation.

stability_decay = 100 * normalized_variance(last_n_attempt_scores)
Or more practically:

stability_decay =
  100 * (
    0.5 * failure_ratio_last_5 +
    0.3 * state_downgrade_events_last_14d +
    0.2 * contradiction_rate
  )
Where contradiction rate means success/fail flipping across similar tasks.

5.3 Latency decay
This catches slowing recall.

latency_decay =
  100 * max(0, recent_normalized_latency_mean - baseline_normalized_latency_mean)
If the student once recalled quickly and now takes longer, risk rises.

5.4 Support decay
This reflects loss of independence.

support_decay =
  100 * max(0, recent_support_dependency - historical_support_dependency_baseline)
5.5 Mode decay
This captures slipping down the recall ladder.

If a student used to succeed in:

free recall

application

but now only in recognition, the system should penalize that heavily.

Map recall modes to numeric rank:

recognition = 3
cued_recall = 4
free_recall = 5
application = 6
transfer = 7
pressure_stable = 8
durable = 9
Then:

mode_decay = 100 * max(0, historical_best_verified_mode - recent_verified_mode) / 6
5.6 Pressure decay
This captures calm-vs-pressure deterioration.

pressure_decay = pressure_gap_score
5.7 Decay status thresholds
0–19   stable
20–39  watchlist
40–59  fragile
60–79  decaying
80–100 collapsed
These thresholds should remain tunable.

6. Recall Mode Classification Engine
Exact rules
The engine should not guess mode from one attempt.
It should infer the highest verified mode and dominant current mode.

6.1 Evidence requirements
Recognizable
Require:

at least 2 correct recognition attempts

no requirement for independent generation

Cued recallable
Require:

at least 2 successful attempts after low/moderate cue

free recall still inconsistent

Free recallable
Require:

at least 2 recent successful no-option/no-hint recall attempts

latency not collapsed

not contradicted by recent repeated free-recall failure

Applicable
Require:

successful concept use in solving or classification tasks

not just stating definition

Transferable
Require:

success in at least 2 variant contexts or rephrasings

Pressure stable
Require:

success under timed or dense mixed conditions

acceptable pressure latency

low freeze/switch behavior

Durable
Require:

successful retrieval preserved across meaningful gaps

no serious state regression over a configured interval

6.2 Verification formula
For each mode, calculate:

mode_evidence_score =
  0.45 * success_rate_in_mode +
  0.20 * independence_score +
  0.15 * latency_quality +
  0.10 * consistency +
  0.10 * recency_weight
Then set thresholds, for example:

>= 0.80 verified
0.60–0.79 emerging
< 0.60 not verified
6.3 Dominant current mode
This is the highest mode with verified evidence in the recent window.

6.4 Highest historical verified mode
This is used for decay analysis.

This distinction is essential:

historical best shows what the student once achieved

dominant current shows what remains true now

7. Failure Diagnosis Engine
Exact decision logic
This engine should be probabilistic or weighted-rule based.

Start rule-based first.
You can later upgrade to ML.

Each failure mode gets a score from evidence.

7.1 True forgetting score
true_forgetting_score =
  0.30 * long_gap_indicator +
  0.20 * all_modes_failure_rate +
  0.20 * cue_nonrecovery_rate +
  0.15 * weak_historical_mastery +
  0.15 * low_recent_exposure
Interpretation:

student once knew little or weakly

long time gap

cannot recover even with cue

7.2 Retrieval blockage score
retrieval_blockage_score =
  0.30 * recognition_success_with_free_recall_failure +
  0.25 * cue_recovery_success +
  0.20 * hesitation_score +
  0.15 * recent_success_history +
  0.10 * “I know this” behavioral markers
7.3 Interference score
interference_score =
  0.40 * intruding_concept_match_rate +
  0.20 * sibling_overlap_proximity +
  0.15 * timed_confusion_spike +
  0.15 * post-new-topic-onset +
  0.10 * contrast-pair history
7.4 Incomplete understanding score
incomplete_understanding_score =
  0.30 * low_application_success_history +
  0.20 * low_explanation_quality +
  0.20 * shallow_pattern_of_memorized_phrasing +
  0.15 * historically weak mastery +
  0.15 * fails_across_all_variants
7.5 Context lock score
context_lock_score =
  0.40 * standard_form_success_but_variant_failure +
  0.25 * reverse_question_failure +
  0.20 * real_world_context_failure +
  0.15 * surface-pattern dependence
7.6 Pressure collapse score
pressure_collapse_score =
  0.45 * pressure_gap_score +
  0.20 * timed_switch_rate +
  0.15 * timed_freeze_rate +
  0.10 * timed_latency_spike +
  0.10 * calm_success_rate
7.7 Confidence disruption score
confidence_disruption_score =
  0.35 * right_to_wrong_switch_rate +
  0.25 * low_confidence_correct_rate +
  0.20 * overchecking_time_excess +
  0.20 * self-report mismatch
7.8 Fragmented knowledge score
fragmented_knowledge_score =
  0.30 * partial_correct_rate +
  0.25 * step_gap_rate +
  0.20 * disconnected_piece_recall_pattern +
  0.15 * procedure_middle-step failures +
  0.10 * explanation incompleteness
7.9 Final diagnosis selection
Normalize all candidate scores and choose:

primary failure mode = highest score

secondary failure mode = second highest if within threshold proximity

Example rule:

if second_score >= 0.80 * first_score
  keep both
else
  keep primary only
This matters because many failures are hybrid.
For example:

interference + confidence disruption

incomplete understanding + fragmentation

8. Memory State Engine
Exact state machine rules
This is one of the most important parts of the system.

A student should move up or down only on evidence.

8.1 State order
unexposed
→ exposed
→ familiar
→ recognizable
→ cued_recallable
→ free_recallable
→ applicable
→ transferable
→ pressure_stable

8.2 Promotion rules
exposed → familiar
Evidence:

at least 1 exposure event

signs of surface recognition or recall attempt

familiar → recognizable
Evidence:

recognition_evidence_score >= 0.80

at least 2 recognition successes

recognizable → cued_recallable
Evidence:

cued_recall_evidence_score >= 0.75

no heavy contradiction in recent attempts

cued_recallable → free_recallable
Evidence:

free_recall_evidence_score >= 0.80

at least 2 independent recent free recalls

free_recallable → applicable
Evidence:

application_evidence_score >= 0.80

at least 2 direct usage successes

success across at least 2 variants

pressure gap below threshold

pressure_stable → durable
Evidence:

maintained across long-gap checks

no meaningful regression for configured duration

8.3 Demotion rules
A unit should not demote after one isolated miss, unless collapse is strong.

Suggested rules:

durable → pressure_stable
If long-gap retention holds but pressure fails.

pressure_stable → applicable
If pressure evidence drops below threshold repeatedly.

applicable → free_recallable
If application fails repeatedly but free recall still works.

free_recallable → recognizable
If free recall fails repeatedly and only recognition remains.

recognizable → familiar
If even recognition becomes unstable.

8.4 Persistence guard
To avoid noisy state jumping, require:

at least 2 confirming events for promotion

at least 2 contradictory events for demotion

unless failure severity is extreme

This is important.
Without it, states will thrash.

These overlays let the coach act without corrupting the core ladder.

9. Interference Mapping Engine
Exact graph logic
This should function as a student-specific confusion graph.

For every wrong attempt, if the wrong answer or wrong explanation maps to a sibling or contrast-pair unit, add confusion evidence.

9.1 Edge update formula
new_confusion_strength =
  old_confusion_strength * decay_factor +
  confusion_event_weight
Where:

decay_factor slowly reduces dormant confusion over time

confusion_event_weight is higher if:

the intruding concept was explicit

it happened in free recall

it happened repeatedly

it happened under time pressure

Example:

confusion_event_weight =
  10
  + 4 if timed
  + 3 if repeated within 7 days
  + 2 if same intruder concept
Cap at 100.

9.2 Edge status rules
0–19   watchlist
20–49  active
50–79  high_risk
80–100 severe_confusion
To stabilize, require successful contrast discrimination.

Each successful contrast session can reduce the edge strength.

9.3 Directionality
If A is often mistaken for B but B is rarely mistaken for A, store that.

That matters for instructional design.

Example:
students may answer “diffusion” when asked “osmosis” much more often than the reverse.

12. Pressure Recall Engine

At first, model only:

pressure_resilience_score =
  100 * (
    0.40 * pressure_accuracy +

pressure_gap_score =

13. Dependency Risk Engine

19. Pseudocode for full recomputation
Here is the simplest clean recomputation path.

function recomputeKnowledgeState(studentId: string, knowledgeUnitId: string) {
  const unit = loadKnowledgeUnit(knowledgeUnitId)
  const attempts = loadRecentAttempts(studentId, knowledgeUnitId)
  const history = loadHistoricalSummary(studentId, knowledgeUnitId)
  const interferenceEdges = loadInterferenceEdges(studentId, knowledgeUnitId)

  const derived = deriveFeatures(unit, attempts, history, interferenceEdges)

  const recallProfile = classifyRecallModes(derived)
  const decayProfile = detectDecay(derived, recallProfile, history)
  const failureProfile = diagnoseFailure(derived, recallProfile, decayProfile, interferenceEdges)
  const pressureProfile = computePressureProfile(derived)
  const dependencyProfile = computeDependencyRisk(unit, studentId)
  const memoryState = resolveMemoryState(recallProfile, decayProfile, pressureProfile, history)

  const interventionPlan = maybePlanIntervention({
    unit,
    recallProfile,
    decayProfile,
    failureProfile,
    pressureProfile,
    dependencyProfile,
    memoryState
  })

  const reviewPlan = scheduleReview({
    unit,
    memoryState,
    decayProfile,
    failureProfile,
    pressureProfile,
    dependencyProfile,
    interventionPlan
  })

  persistStudentKnowledgeState(...)
  persistInterventionPlanIfNeeded(...)
  persistReviewPlan(...)
}
That is the core heartbeat of the layer.

1. System role inside the larger academic platform
This subsystem sits between the learning interactions and the coach decision layer.

Upstream systems feeding it
question player

diagnostic engine

quizzes and topic tests

teach mode

glossary drills

formula drills

mixed review sessions

timed mock system

intervention sessions

oral/audio recall modules

Downstream systems consuming it
coach hub

daily mission planner

session builder

parent notifications

teacher/super-admin analytics

content quality engine

question selection engine

timing intelligence

sequencing intelligence

So this system is not just passive storage.
It is a memory intelligence core.

2. High-level service decomposition
A clean decomposition would be:

A. Attempt Ingestion Service
Receives raw interaction events.

B. Memory State Service
Owns current StudentKnowledgeState.

C. Feature Derivation Service
Computes rolling and mode-specific features.

D. Recall Intelligence Service
Runs:

recall mode classification

decay detection

failure diagnosis

pressure analysis

interference updates

E. Intervention Service
Chooses and stores intervention plans.

F. Review Scheduling Service
Maintains upcoming review queue.

G. Coach Directive Service
Produces actionable outputs for the coach brain.

H. Analytics Service
Aggregates cohort and curriculum-level patterns.

You can combine some of these early, but this separation is the correct logical boundary.

3. Database design
You need both transactional tables and event tables.

The safest setup is:

PostgreSQL for transactional/state tables

optional event stream or append-only audit table

Redis for fast queueing/caching if needed

background job system for recomputation tasks

4. Core relational schema
I will define the main tables.

4.1 knowledge_units
This is the canonical catalog of trackable learning units.

CREATE TABLE knowledge_units (
  id UUID PRIMARY KEY,
  subject_id UUID NOT NULL,
  topic_id UUID NOT NULL,
  subtopic_id UUID NULL,

  title TEXT NOT NULL,
  canonical_label TEXT NOT NULL,
  description TEXT NULL,

  importance_weight NUMERIC(4,3) NOT NULL DEFAULT 0.500,
  dependency_weight NUMERIC(4,3) NOT NULL DEFAULT 0.500,
  confusion_proneness NUMERIC(4,3) NOT NULL DEFAULT 0.000,
  exam_frequency_weight NUMERIC(4,3) NOT NULL DEFAULT 0.500,

  canonical_representations JSONB NOT NULL DEFAULT '{}'::jsonb,
  tags JSONB NOT NULL DEFAULT '[]'::jsonb,

  created_at TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW(),
  updated_at TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW()
);
4.2 knowledge_unit_edges
This stores graph relationships.

CREATE TABLE knowledge_unit_edges (
  id UUID PRIMARY KEY,
  source_unit_id UUID NOT NULL REFERENCES knowledge_units(id),
  target_unit_id UUID NOT NULL REFERENCES knowledge_units(id),
  edge_type TEXT NOT NULL, -- prerequisite, sibling, contrast_pair, related, supports_application_of
  weight NUMERIC(4,3) NOT NULL DEFAULT 0.500,

  created_at TIMESTAMPTZ NOT NULL DEFAULT NOW(),
  UNIQUE(source_unit_id, target_unit_id, edge_type)
);
This table is critical because dependency and interference intelligence rely on it.

5. Service-by-service responsibilities
Now let us define what each service owns.

5.1 Attempt Ingestion Service
Responsibility
Accept raw attempt data from the frontend or gameplay layer and persist it cleanly.

It should do
validate payload

map question to knowledge_unit_id

infer mode and format

write retrieval_attempts

emit retrieval_attempt_recorded event

enqueue recomputation job

It should not do
heavy scoring

intervention planning

review scheduling directly

Keep it fast and reliable.

5.2 Memory State Service
Responsibility
Own the materialized truth in student_knowledge_states.

It should do
create state row if missing

apply recomputed values

write state transitions

update counters

maintain flags

It should not do
raw ingestion

frontend orchestration

analytics aggregation

5.3 Feature Derivation Service
Responsibility
Convert raw attempts into derived metrics.

Inputs
recent retrieval attempts

historical state

knowledge unit metadata

interference edges

Outputs
A structured feature bundle like:

type DerivedFeatureBundle = {
  studentId: string
  knowledgeUnitId: string

  recentAccuracy: number
  rollingAccuracy: number
  recognitionAccuracy: number
  freeRecallAccuracy: number
  applicationAccuracy: number
  transferAccuracy: number
  pressureAccuracy: number

  latencyQuality: number
  recentNormalizedLatency: number
  supportDependencyScore: number
  confidenceCalibrationScore: number
  pressureGapScore: number

  interferenceRate: number
  dominantIntruderConceptId?: string

  consistencyScore: number
  contradictionRate: number
  cueRecoveryRate: number
  stateRegressionCount14d: number
}
This should be reusable by all scoring engines.

5.4 Recall Intelligence Service
Responsibility
Run the scoring logic.

This is where you execute:

recall mode classification

decay detection

failure diagnosis

pressure profile

dependency risk

interference update

Output
A single decision bundle:

type RecallIntelligenceBundle = {
  recallProfile: ...
  decayProfile: ...
  failureProfile: ...
  pressureProfile: ...
  dependencyProfile: ...
  interferenceUpdates: ...
  proposedMemoryState: ...
}
5.5 Intervention Service
Responsibility
Select, create, update, or cancel intervention plans.

It should do
inspect failure mode

inspect current state

inspect recent plan failures

create new plan or revise active one

attach retest strategy

It should also
avoid duplication.
If a suitable active plan already exists, it should update rather than create a new conflicting plan.

5.6 Review Scheduling Service
Responsibility
Maintain the review queue.

It should do
compute urgency

assign due time

choose review mode

reschedule after completed reviews

mark missed review patterns

rebalance overload

Important
This service must understand the student’s total review burden.
If 70 reviews are due tomorrow, that is bad scheduling.

So it needs queue shaping rules.

5.7 Coach Directive Service
Responsibility
Translate engine outputs into coach actions.

Examples:

show rescue card now

insert contrast block into next session

change tomorrow’s mission to foundation repair

hold off on new content

notify parent of fragile prerequisite

prompt super-admin content audit if cohort-wide issue

This is where memory intelligence becomes product behavior.

7. Background job architecture
This subsystem must be job-driven.
Do not do all recomputation inline on user request.

Use a job system like:

Go workers + Redis queue

or a proper message bus if the platform grows

Key jobs:

7.1 recompute_knowledge_state_job
Triggered after:

retrieval attempt

intervention completion

manual recompute

nightly consistency pass

Steps:

load recent attempts

derive features

classify recall mode

detect decay

diagnose failure

update interference edges

compute pressure/dependency

resolve new memory state

update state row

write transition if changed

enqueue intervention/scheduling jobs if needed

7.2 plan_intervention_job
Triggered when:

state becomes fragile/decaying/collapsed

failure mode significant

active interference severe

pressure gap severe

regression detected

Steps:

inspect current state

inspect recent intervention history

choose family

generate steps

generate retest plan

persist intervention plan

7.3 schedule_review_job
Triggered when:

intervention plan created/completed

decay score changes

state changes

exam proximity changes

missed review detected

Steps:

compute urgency

compute mode

assign due window

rebalance against existing queue

persist/update schedule item

7.4 nightly_decay_scan_job
Runs daily.

Purpose:

find units whose time-gap alone now raises decay risk

create review items even without new attempts

downgrade stale durable illusions where needed

surface dormant risk before the student re-fails in public

This is essential.
Otherwise the system only reacts after failure.

7.5 interference_graph_decay_job
Runs daily or weekly.

Purpose:

gradually reduce confusion strength for stabilized pairs

prevent old interference edges from staying permanently inflated

preserve severe active pairs longer

7.6 review_queue_shaping_job
Runs periodically.

Purpose:

smooth review overload

move low-priority maintenance items later

protect urgent foundational reviews

maintain a sensible daily workload

8. Real-time vs asynchronous execution
You need a hybrid model.

Real-time
Immediately after attempt submission:

store attempt

return success

maybe do very light local feedback if needed

Asynchronous
Within seconds:

recompute state

create intervention

adjust review queue

emit coach directive

This keeps gameplay responsive while preserving intelligence.

9. Execution timeline after an attempt
Here is the exact end-to-end flow.

Step 1: Student answers a question
Frontend submits attempt payload.

Step 2: Attempt Ingestion Service
validates payload

writes retrieval_attempts

emits event

enqueues recompute_knowledge_state_job

Step 3: Recompute job runs
loads state + recent attempts + unit graph context

derives features

computes scores

updates interference

resolves memory state

writes student_knowledge_states

writes transition event if state changed

Step 4: Decision branching
If risk high or regression present:

enqueue plan_intervention_job

enqueue schedule_review_job

Step 5: Coach Directive Service
Reads updated state and produces a directive such as:

“insert contrast micro-drill now”

“mark for tomorrow’s review”

“parent alert candidate”

“foundation risk: pause next topic”

Step 6: UI reflects change
Coach Hub can now show:

fragile

interference alert

pressure vulnerability

recommended next action

That is the heartbeat.

13. Parent notification logic
This should be very careful.
Do not notify parents about every memory fluctuation.

Good parent notification triggers:

high-dependency unit becomes decaying/collapsed

repeated relapse in same foundational concept

severe pressure vulnerability close to exams

repeated missed review items on critical units

major gap between calm performance and test performance

Example parent alert rule:

if downstreamRiskScore >= 70
and decayStatus in {decaying, collapsed}
and reviewUrgencyScore >= 75
then parent_alert_candidate = true

15. Consistency and idempotency rules
This matters a lot in real systems.

Attempt ingestion
If the frontend retries the same attempt, you must avoid duplicate writes.

Use:

client-generated attempt id

or idempotency key

Recompute jobs
Should be idempotent.
Running recompute twice should produce the same final state.

Review scheduling
Should upsert logically, not spray duplicates.

Example:

one active schedule item per student + knowledge unit + review reason band

Intervention plans
Should avoid multiple simultaneous conflicting plans for the same unit.

16. Concurrency concerns
This subsystem will face races.

Example:

student answers 3 questions on same concept in quick succession

3 recompute jobs fire

You need a strategy:

per studentId + knowledgeUnitId lock

or optimistic concurrency with version number

A clean approach:

state row has version

recompute job reads version

update only if version unchanged

otherwise rerun with fresh data

This prevents stale writes.

18. Review queue shaping rules
This is important enough to define explicitly.

Rule 1
High-dependency fragile units outrank low-dependency maintenance reviews.

Rule 2
Recent recoveries should be revisited soon, but not crowd out urgent collapses.

Rule 3
No daily queue should exceed the student’s configured capacity.

Rule 4
If overload occurs, defer:

low urgency

durable maintenance

low importance

low exam frequency

Rule 5
Interference pairs should often be scheduled together in contrast mode, not separately.

That last rule is a big one.

23. Decision hierarchy
The coach should not let every engine act independently.
The orchestrator needs a priority hierarchy.

A strong hierarchy is:

foundation risk

active collapse

severe interference

pressure collapse near exams

recent recovery confirmation

ordinary maintenance

new content progression

That means new teaching should often lose to memory rescue when the rescue is high leverage.

24. Failure recovery policy
The system should also know what to do when interventions fail repeatedly.

Example escalation ladder:

First failure
retry same intervention with slightly different examples

Second failure
escalate to deeper family

Third failure
mark as persistent weakness, widen diagnosis

Fourth failure
trigger broader dependency scan or teach-mode rebuild

This prevents the coach from hammering the same ineffective tactic repeatedly.

25. Exact orchestration policy for persistent weakness
When a concept remains weak after repeated attempts, the system should ask:

is the real problem upstream?

is the contrast pair unresolved?

is the question form too narrow?

is the student overloaded?

is pressure making diagnosis look worse than calm competence?

does the student need a reconstruction sequence rather than more drills?

This is where the memory subsystem should collaborate with:

dependency engine

sequencing engine

adaptation engine

tension map

3.4 Accuracy computation
Use weighted averaging so recent attempts matter more.

func weightedAccuracy(attempts []RetrievalAttempt) float64 {
    if len(attempts) == 0 {
        return 0
    }

    totalWeight := 0.0
    weightedSum := 0.0

    for i, a := range attempts {
        weight := 1.0 + float64(len(attempts)-i)*0.15
        weightedSum += weight * a.RawScore
        totalWeight += weight
    }

    return weightedSum / totalWeight
}

3.5 Consistency score
You want to penalize oscillation.

func consistencyScore(scores []float64) float64 {
    if len(scores) < 2 {
        return 0.5
    }

    mean := avg(scores)
    variance := 0.0
    for _, s := range scores {
        d := s - mean
        variance += d * d
    }
    variance /= float64(len(scores))

    normalizedVariance := math.Min(1.0, variance/0.25)
    return 1.0 - normalizedVariance
}
High consistency means stable behavior.

3.6 Latency normalization
Never compare raw milliseconds across all contexts.

Use an expected time model.

func expectedResponseTimeMs(mode RecallMode, format AttemptFormat, difficulty float64) float64 {
    base := 4000.0

    switch mode {
    case RecallModeRecognition:
        base = 3500
    case RecallModeCuedRecall:
        base = 5000
    case RecallModeFreeRecall:
        base = 7000
    case RecallModeApplication:
        base = 12000
    case RecallModeTransfer:
        base = 14000
    case RecallModePressure:
        base = 8000
    }

    switch format {
    case AttemptFormatWorkedSolution:
        base *= 1.6
    case AttemptFormatTypedLong:
        base *= 1.3
    case AttemptFormatMCQ:
        base *= 0.8
    }

    base *= (0.8 + difficulty*0.6)
    return base
}
Then:

func normalizedLatency(attempt RetrievalAttempt, difficulty float64) float64 {
    if attempt.ResponseTimeMs == nil {
        return 1.0
    }

    expected := expectedResponseTimeMs(attempt.Mode, attempt.Format, difficulty)
    return float64(*attempt.ResponseTimeMs) / expected
}
Latency quality:

func latencyQuality(norm float64) float64 {
    if norm <= 1.0 {
        return 1.0
    }
    score := 1.0 - ((norm - 1.0) / 2.0)
    return clamp(score, 0, 1)
}
3.7 Support dependency
This should combine several signs of reliance.

func supportDependencyScore(attempts []RetrievalAttempt) float64 {
    if len(attempts) == 0 {
        return 0
    }

    hintsRate := ratio(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool { return a.HintsUsed > 0 }, attempts)

    optionVisibleSuccess := successRate(filter(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool { return a.OptionsVisible }, attempts))
    noOptionSuccess := successRate(filter(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool { return !a.OptionsVisible }, attempts))
    optionGap := math.Max(0, optionVisibleSuccess-noOptionSuccess)

    formulaVisibleSuccess := successRate(filter(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool { return a.FormulaBankVisible }, attempts))
    formulaHiddenSuccess := successRate(filter(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool { return !a.FormulaBankVisible }, attempts))
    formulaGap := math.Max(0, formulaVisibleSuccess-formulaHiddenSuccess)

    cueRecoveryRate := cueRecovery(attempts)

    return clamp(
        35*hintsRate+
            30*optionGap+
            20*formulaGap+
            15*cueRecoveryRate,
        0, 100,
    )
}

func interferenceFeatures(attempts []RetrievalAttempt) (rate float64, timedRate float64, dominant *uuid.UUID, dominance float64) {
    wrongAttempts := filter(func(a RetrievalAttempt) bool {
        return a.Correctness == CorrectnessIncorrect
    }, attempts)

    if len(wrongAttempts) == 0 {
        return 0, 0, nil, 0
    }

    intruderCounts := map[uuid.UUID]int{}
    timedIntrusions := 0
    totalIntrusions := 0

    for _, a := range wrongAttempts {
        if a.IntrudingConceptID != nil {
            intruderCounts[*a.IntrudingConceptID]++
            totalIntrusions++
            if a.Timed {
                timedIntrusions++
            }
        }
    }

    if totalIntrusions == 0 {
        return 0, 0, nil, 0
    }

    domID, domCount := maxIntruder(intruderCounts)
    return float64(totalIntrusions) / float64(len(wrongAttempts)),
        float64(timedIntrusions) / float64(len(wrongAttempts)),
        &domID,
        float64(domCount) / float64(totalIntrusions)
}

func computeTimeDecay(features DerivedFeatureBundle, historical HistoricalStats, state StudentKnowledgeState) float64 {
    masteryBuffer := 1.0 + float64(features.RecentIndependentSuccesses)*0.35 + stateBonusDays(state.MemoryState)*0.2
    gapRaw := features.DaysSinceLastSuccess / masteryBuffer

    threshold := safeGapThreshold(state.MemoryState, historical)

5.6 Engine pseudocode
func (e *DefaultDecayEngine) Detect(...) (DecayProfile, error) {
    t := computeTimeDecay(features, in.HistoricalStats, in.CurrentState)
    s := computeStabilityDecay(features, in.HistoricalStats)
    l := computeLatencyDecay(features, in.CurrentState)
    sup := computeSupportDecay(features, in.CurrentState)
    m := computeModeDecay(recall)
    p := computePressureDecay(features)

    total := totalDecayScore(t, s, l, sup, m, p)
    status := statusFromScore(total)

    return DecayProfile{
        TimeDecay: t,
        StabilityDecay: s,
        LatencyDecay: l,
        SupportDecay: sup,
        ModeDecay: m,
        PressureDecay: p,
        TotalScore: total,
        Status: status,
        ReactivationNeeded: status >= DecayStatusFragile,
        UrgentReviewNeeded: total >= 60,
        PressureCheckNeeded: p >= 25,
        ConfusionCheckNeeded: features.InterferenceRate >= 0.35,
    }, nil
}

func trueForgettingScore(features DerivedFeatureBundle, recall RecallProfile, historical HistoricalStats) float64 {
    longGap := clamp(features.DaysSinceLastSuccess/14.0, 0, 1)
    allModesFailure := 1.0 - maxFloat(
        recall.RecognitionEvidence,
        recall.CuedRecallEvidence,
        recall.FreeRecallEvidence,
    )
    cueNonRecovery := 1.0 - features.CueRecoveryRate
    weakHistory := 1.0
    if modeRank(historical.HistoricalBestVerifiedMode) >= modeRank(RecallModeFreeRecall) {
        weakHistory = 0.3
    }
    lowExposure := clamp(1.0-float64(historical.TotalAttempts)/8.0, 0, 1)

    return clamp(
        0.30*longGap+
            0.20*allModesFailure+
            0.20*cueNonRecovery+
            0.15*weakHistory+
            0.15*lowExposure,
        0, 1,
    )
}

func choosePrimarySecondary(scores map[FailureMode]float64) (FailureMode, *FailureMode, float64) {
    ordered := sortFailureScores(scores)
    primary := ordered[0]
    var secondary *FailureMode

    if len(ordered) > 1 && ordered[1].Score >= 0.80*ordered[0].Score {

func applyPersistenceGuard(current, proposed StudentMemoryState, in EngineInput, decay DecayProfile) StudentMemoryState {
    if proposed == current {
        return current
    }

    if proposed > current {
        if in.HistoricalStats.TotalAttempts < 2 {
            return current
        }
        return proposed
    }

    if proposed < current {
        if decay.TotalScore < 45 && in.HistoricalStats.StateRegressions14d == 0 {
            return current
        }
        return proposed
    }

    return proposed
}
You can make this more precise later, but the principle stands:

promotions need repeated evidence

demotions need repeated contradiction, unless collapse is strong

7.6 Overlay flags
Flags are critical because not all nuance belongs in the ladder itself.

func deriveFlags(features DerivedFeatureBundle, decay DecayProfile, failure FailureProfile, pressure PressureProfile, dependency DependencyProfile) []string {
    flags := []string{}

    if decay.Status >= DecayStatusFragile {
        flags = append(flags, "fragile")
    }
    if failure.Primary == FailureModeInterference {
        flags = append(flags, "interference_prone")
    }
    if failure.Primary == FailureModePressureCollapse || features.PressureGapScore >= 25 {
        flags = append(flags, "pressure_vulnerable")
    }
    if dependency.DownstreamRiskScore >= 70 {
        flags = append(flags, "high_dependency")
    }
    if decay.TotalScore < 40 && features.HasRecentRecovery {
        flags = append(flags, "recovering")
    }
    if decay.TotalScore >= 60 && features.HasRecentRegression {
        flags = append(flags, "relapsing")
    }

    return flags
}

func interventionPriority(decay DecayProfile, dependency DependencyProfile, exam ExamContext, current StudentKnowledgeState) float64 {
    relapseRisk := 0.0
    if contains(current.Flags, "relapsing") {
        relapseRisk = 1.0
    }

    return clamp(
        0.25*decay.TotalScore+
            0.20*dependency.DownstreamRiskScore+
            0.20*exam.UrgencyScore+
            0.15*(current.ReviewUrgencyScore)+
            0.10*current.InterferenceRiskScore+
            0.10*(relapseRisk*100),
        0, 100,
    )

10.4 Core selection logic
func (e *DefaultInterventionEngine) Plan(...) (*InterventionPlanDraft, error) {
    if decay.Status == DecayStatusStable &&
        failure.Primary == FailureModeUnknown &&
        pressure.GapScore < 20 {
        return nil, nil
    }

    family := failure.InterventionFamily

    if shouldEscalate(in.ActivePlans, in.HistoricalStats) {
        family = escalateFamily(family, failure.Primary)
    }

    steps := buildStepsForFamily(family, in, failure, memory, pressure)
    retest := buildRetestPlan(family, failure, memory)
    priority := interventionPriority(decay, dependency, in.ExamContext, in.CurrentState)

    draft := &InterventionPlanDraft{
        Family: family,
        Reason: buildReason(failure, decay, pressure),
        PriorityScore: priority,
        TargetState: targetStateForFamily(family, memory.NewState),
        Steps: steps,
        RetestPlan: retest,
        EstimatedMinutes: estimateDuration(family),
    }

    return draft, nil
}

12.2 Full pseudocode
func (s *DefaultRecomputeService) RecomputeKnowledgeUnit(ctx context.Context, studentID, knowledgeUnitID uuid.UUID) error {
    in, err := s.loader.LoadEngineInput(ctx, studentID, knowledgeUnitID)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    features, err := s.featureDeriver.Derive(ctx, in)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    recall, err := s.recallEngine.Classify(ctx, in, features)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    decay, err := s.decayEngine.Detect(ctx, in, features, recall)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    failure, err := s.failureEngine.Diagnose(ctx, in, features, recall, decay)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    pressure, err := s.pressureEngine.Compute(ctx, in, features)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    dependency, err := s.dependencyEngine.Compute(ctx, in)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    memory, err := s.memoryStateEngine.Resolve(ctx, in, features, recall, decay, failure, pressure)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    plan, err := s.interventionEngine.Plan(ctx, in, recall, decay, failure, memory, pressure, dependency)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    review, err := s.schedulingEngine.Schedule(ctx, in, decay, failure, memory, pressure, dependency, plan)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }

    tx, err := s.repo.BeginTx(ctx)
    if err != nil {
        return err
    }
    defer tx.Rollback()

    if err := s.stateRepo.UpsertKnowledgeState(ctx, tx, buildUpdatedState(in, features, recall, decay, failure, pressure, dependency, memory, plan, review)); err != nil {
        return err
    }

    if memory.Promoted || memory.Demoted {
        if err := s.transitionRepo.Insert(ctx, tx, buildTransitionEvent(in, memory, features, recall, decay, failure)); err != nil {
            return err
        }
    }

    if err := s.interferenceRepo.ApplyDerivedChanges(ctx, tx, features, in); err != nil {
        return err
    }

    if plan != nil {
        if err := s.planRepo.UpsertDraft(ctx, tx, in.StudentID, in.KnowledgeUnit.ID, *plan); err != nil {
            return err
        }
    }

    if review != nil {
        if err := s.reviewRepo.UpsertDecision(ctx, tx, in.StudentID, in.KnowledgeUnit.ID, *review); err != nil {
            return err
        }
    }

    if err := tx.Commit(); err != nil {
        return err
    }

    return s.directiveEmitter.Emit(ctx, buildDirective(in, memory, decay, failure, pressure, dependency, plan, review))
}

16. Edge cases you must design for
Sparse history

stay conservative

Conflicting evidence
If recall profile is noisy:

keep state stable

attach fragile/review flags

do not over-promote

No pressure data yet
Do not label pressure-vulnerable unless evidence exists.

Artificial support
If question interface automatically shows formulas/options, that attempt should not be treated as independent recall.

One lucky correct guess
Must not promote state.

Multi-concept question contamination

5.3 Block selection rules
The builder should obey these rules:

Rule A: foundation before expansion
If a high-dependency concept is decaying, new downstream teaching should usually pause.

Rule B: rescue before repetition
If the failure is concept confusion, do not just assign more standard questions. Insert a contrast block.

Rule C: confirm recovery
If a concept recovered recently, schedule a confirmation block instead of assuming the recovery is real.

Rule D: pressure weakness late in session
Pressure probes should usually come after calm success, not at the very beginning.

Rule E: one session should not become pure rescue overload
If too many weak concepts exist, select the highest-leverage subset and defer lower-value maintenance.

11. End-to-end event flow
Now let us define the canonical brain event flow.

Flow A — Retrieval attempt
student answers a question

attempt event is recorded

Brain Update Service updates raw evidence

Brain Orchestration Service loads current student academic state

recall, decay, failure, interference, pressure, dependency engines run

policy engine resolves priority

session/mission/directive composers create actions

updated state and explanation are persisted

Coach Hub and relevant surfaces refresh

That is the standard learning feedback loop.

Flow B — Session completion
session ends

session outcome event is recorded

state deltas are computed

recovery confirmations, fragile units, and pressure outcomes are updated

mission and review queue are recalculated

debrief is composed

next-day mission posture is adjusted

Flow C — Intervention completion
intervention block finishes

intervention state is updated

immediate retest or confirmation is evaluated

memory state may promote, remain fragile, or relapse

follow-up review timing is recalculated

adaptation profile is updated based on intervention success/failure

Flow D — Nightly scan
dormant state is scanned for time-based decay

watchlist and fragile candidates are updated

due review items are generated

queue load is shaped

new priority directives may be created for next day

This is how the brain remains proactive rather than only reactive.

14. Implementation boundaries
To keep the system sane, enforce these boundaries:

State components
Own persisted academic truth.

Interpretation engines
Read truth, produce interpreted outputs, but do not directly mutate product surfaces.

Policy engine
Resolves what should happen, but does not build UI artifacts.

Capability registry
Defines what tools exist, but does not decide when to use them.

Experience composers
Build sessions/missions/cards from decisions and capabilities, but do not invent academic policy.

Communication composers
Translate outputs for audiences, but do not reinterpret the student academically.

These boundaries are important.

11. The real system boundary
One crucial point:

The Academic Coach Brain should not own raw content ingestion, raw question generation, or general UI rendering.

It should own:

student truth

academic interpretation

academic priority resolution

capability-aware composition

translated outputs

That keeps the boundary clean.

12. Canonical final statement for engineering
This is the compressed implementation-level definition:

The Academic Coach Brain is a state-driven orchestration system that maintains a live academic model of each learner, interprets evidence through specialized intelligence engines, resolves priorities through explicit policy, and composes the next best academic experience using a registry of available learning capabilities and assets.

That is the build target.

13. The next proper move
The next strongest step is to convert this implementation matrix into a build roadmap by workstream, for example:

Workstream A: State + events

Workstream B: Memory and diagnosis engines

Workstream C: Policy + directives

Workstream D: Session and mission composition

Workstream E: Parent/admin intelligence

Workstream F: Content and question integrations

And then define:

dependencies

milestones

parallelizable tasks

acceptance criteria for each workstream

That would make this executable as a real development program.
